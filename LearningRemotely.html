Welcome

Education at McMaster is undergoing an unprecedented change. With classes for the fall term shifting online, McMaster students are faced with the challenge of learning remotely in a fully online environment. In navigating these changes, it is important to remember that you are not alone on this journey; staff and faculty at McMaster have spent a tremendous amount of time and care to ensure that you are able to learn well online.

The Learning Remotely site is a collection of resources intended to support you as you transition to an online learning environment. While the shift to remote education may be temporary, good habits and practices will remain. Use this as an opportunity to set up an efficient learning space, learn how to manage your time, practice effective study strategies, and maintain your well-being. Don’t hesitate to seek support from McMaster faculty, staff, and other students who are here to help you succeed. Together we can and will turn these challenges into opportunities towards a brighter world!

Before you get started, keep these principles in mind as you work through your online learning.

Be Patient

  • Don’t be afraid to reach out for help from people who are offering their support
  • Check in with students and peers to offer encouragement and share insights
  • Offer to make connections between those looking for help and those who are providing it

Be Confident

  • Effective online experiences can be created without sacrificing core learning needs
  • Focus on what is most necessary to successfully complete the term
  • Keep content and assessments simple, inclusive, and accessible
  • No need to develop state-of-the-art learning materials right now

Use Available Support

  • Everyone is learning new tools and skills while dealing with these difficult circumstances
  • Show compassion, care, and flexibility with students and colleagues
  • Prioritize communication and transparency over perfection to help alleviate uncertainty and ensure others feel supported

IT Continuity Tools & Services

  • Find a spot where you can comfortably focus

    Designate this area as a ‘workspace’ in order to distinguish it from zones for relaxing and entertainment. Get creative, especially if your options are limited, and discover what works for you!

    Consider the ergonomics of your workspace and consult McMaster’s Computer Workstation Self-Adjustment Resource for guidance. Take frequent stretch breaks to minimize discomfort so you can continue working comfortably.


  • Declutter Your Physical and Digital Environments

    Clutter affects your brain and your work, leading to stress, anxiety, and procrastination. Reduce the amount of unnecessary items in your workspace by establishing organization systems that allow you to control the flow of new files or downloads and quickly access what you’re looking for.

    Consider setting up a separate ‘work account’ on your computer and sort individual files into folders according to a ‘root’ system (i.e. General Folder -> Specific Folder -> Specific Files).


  • Control interruptions and distractions

    Entering into a ‘flow-state’ will allow you to study more effectively and productively, but distractive elements in your environment can inhibit your focus and hamper learning. Communicate your ‘study schedule’ (i.e. times during the day when you need to focus without interruption) with the people in your living environment, including your friends, so they know when not to (and when to) disturb you.

    Consider managing the notification settings on your devices and using a time-tracking app to limit technological distractions.

Technology Checklist

  • Internet:

    An internet speed of 6 Mbps or higher is recommended for students. You can check your internet speed online using a variety of sites.

  • Computer:

    Whether it be a desktop, laptop, or tablet ensure that your device of choice has the power, capacity, and versatility to handle your personal workload. Consider upgrading your existing set up with a keyboard, mouse, and monitor to increase the efficiency and ergonomics of your workspace.

  • Browser:

    Microsoft and Apple computers come pre-installed web browsers Edge and Safari. While these are solid tools that will get the job done, alternative browsers such as Chrome and Firefox offer a substantial upgrade in terms of features and capabilities at no cost.

  • Software:

    As a McMaster student you have free and unlimited access to the Office 365 suite, including Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Teams, and more, as well as the Google G-Suite, which includes Gmail, Calendar, Docs, and Google Drive.

  • Video:

    A webcam comes built-in to most laptops and may be necessary for certain courses that require a live video feed for quizzes, tests, and exams.

  • Audio:

    Sound quality greatly affects our ability to focus, pay attention, and understand. Consider noise cancelling headphones or headphones with a built-in microphone. Additionally, a stand-alone microphone would dramatically improve your audio output.

  • Macromanage Your Time

    Use a calendar—whether it be a physical calendar or an app such as Google calendar—to schedule important dates and deadlines for your courses as soon as this information is available. This gives you a picture of how much time you have to work on a given task, allowing you to schedule time incrementally to complete it. Make it a habit to check your calendar regularly so due dates don’t catch you by surprise.

    Using the knowledge of important dates and deadlines, you can work towards the completion of tasks well in advance over a longer period of time. Essay due in two weeks? Schedule an hour today to work on brainstorming ideas, and three hours tomorrow to begin a rough draft. By giving yourself less time to complete a task you are getting more done, giving you significantly more free time to do things that you want to do.

  • Micromanage Your Time

    Once you’re able to see the bigger picture you can begin to consider how, what, and when you will study down to the minute. Use your calendar to practice Time Blocking which involves planning out every moment of your day in advance and dedicating specific time “blocks” for certain tasks and responsibilities. If scheduling every minute of your day sounds tedious and robotic, consider how productive you would be without any important dates or deadlines. When your day looks like a blank slate, you open the door for the many distractions in your environment to fill in the blanks and take control of your time.

    Consider how your time will be managed within ‘blocks’ of tasks and responsibilities. For example, if you’ve given yourself 3 hours to study for an upcoming test how will that time be managed? One popular method is the Pomodoro technique, which breaks down work into timed intervals separated by short and long breaks. An alternative to this strategy is FlowTime, which encourages deep focus by allowing you to get into the ‘zone’. Experiment with these tools and find a method that works for you!

  • Schedule things that you want to do along with things that you have to do

    The key to making time management an enduring habit is your perspective. If you see a schedule as a tyrant, punishing you with unpleasant tasks and responsibilities then you are more likely to avoid managing your time altogether. If, however, you use time management to design the ideal day that you would like to have, given what you have to do and what you want to do, then your schedule becomes a benevolent aid with your best interests in mind.

  • Attend Lectures

    Treat lectures as you would an in-person classroom. Come prepared, ready to learn, and participate. Make the effort to reduce distractive agents in your environment (i.e. your phone, computer) to help you focus. If the lectures for a course are recorded you may choose to watch them asynchronously (i.e. not live) at a designated time, but keep in mind that you may be missing out on participative elements of the course (which can be mandatory!).

  • Actively Engage

    During lectures by asking questions and taking organized notes that you can refer back to. Before asking a question, discern if the question is appropriate in the given context. For example, asking about grade weight distribution in the middle of a lecture would be inappropriately addressed, and better suited as a question to be asked over email or during office hours. Ensure that you are following appropriate netiquette through online communication.

  • Test Yourself

    Re-reading and re-watching material is a form of rote, mechanical learning that provides you with a shallow understanding of material without a method to evaluate your knowledge. Developing and answering questions from course material is a form of ‘active recall’ that triggers ‘elaborative retrieval processes’, enhancing the mental organization of information which leads to greater retention and testing performance.

    Use flash card apps like Ankii, Brainscape, and Quizlet that utilize cognitive principles such as interleaved practice and spaced repetition, providing you with appropriate questions based on your understanding. Try to create your own flashcards whenever possible. Mix pictures and words and use mnemonic devices to create mental connections. Write questions that stimulate ‘remembering’ (i.e. I know and understand what this means, and avoid questions that reward ‘recognition’ (i.e. “I’ve seen this before”). Challenge yourself and make it an enjoyable experience!

  • Create and Discuss

    Highlighting and underlining study material is a form of pseudo-work which gives you the illusion that you’re learning. In reality, these strategies waste valuable time and ink, inhibiting your learning in the process. Instead, try putting the textbooks, notes, and videos away and summarize and/or discuss what you understand from the material in your own words. This allows you to actively engage with your memory and organize the information into comprehensible parts, which consolidates your understanding by making it more personable and relatable.

    Consider YouTube channels such as Minute Physics (link) or Neuroscientifically Challenged (link) that take difficult topics and teach them in creative, easy to follow ways. If you’re not able to explain the things you’re learning in simple terms that are comprehensible by others, how well do you really understand the material? Learning through instruction can help both you and your peers who might benefit from seeing things in a different way.

  • Distribute Your Study Sessions

    Cramming is the inevitable result of consistent procrastination that takes place during a concentrated period of time before a deadline. Given two weeks to study for a test, most students will only begin studying one or two days before, giving 100% of their effort to 7% of the available time. Why? Since school work is difficult, challenging, and generally less pleasant than other easily accessible and enjoyable activities in our immediate environment, we tend to avoid these unpleasant tasks for as long as possible, attempting to minimize the amount of time we spend doing them.

    Unfortunately habitual cramming overworks the brain, increasing feelings of anxiety, frustration, and fatigue which, combined with reduced sleep and poor diet, can result in detrimental effects on your physical health.

    Additionally, negative associations with schoolwork inhibit learning by preventing us from engaging with difficult material on a deeper, more meaningful level.

    In order to combat procrastination and cramming consider the following:

    • Procrastination is natural and normal; don’t blame yourself. Practice self-compassion by treating yourself with kindness and understanding in the face of mistakes and failures.
    • Recognize when you are procrastinating and consider the thoughts, attitudes, and feelings between you and your schoolwork. Consider how your perspective relates to your greater goals and ambitions. Can you find enjoyment in the material you’re learning? Do you feel rewarded by the challenges of your work?
    • Make the effort to reduce procrastination by distributing your schoolwork over a longer period of time. Consider that if you were to spend between 8-14 hours cramming in the days leading up to a deadline, this time could have been easily broken down into one-hour segments per day over the course of two weeks. In most cases, this works out to be a more efficient use of your time as you are much less productive working 8 hours straight than you are working 8 hours over 8 days.

Meditation Circle

Join Open Circle to learn about meditation and to support each other in becoming more mindful. Each session begins with a check-in about our experience of the past week, then a meditation practice is introduced and guided for 15-20 minutes of practice, followed by an opportunity to discuss any questions or insights. Each week we explore a different mindfulness practice including breath awareness, guided imagery, artistic expression, and other activities that the group wishes to learn together. No experience necessary – whether you are new to meditation or already engaging in regular reflection, having the support of a group can help to strengthen your practice.
